# Verification: Content Words vs Style Words (Kobak et al. 2025)

## Claim
"The method distinguishes content words (topic-driven nouns, e.g. COVID terminology, which spike naturally) from style words (verbs/adjectives whose frequency jumped after LLMs), using the latter as authorship fingerprints rather than semantic signal."

## Verdict: NOT REFUTED (confirmed)

## Source
Kobak, D. et al. "Delving into LLM-assisted writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary." *Science Advances* (July 4, 2025). DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.adt3813. Preprint: arXiv:2406.07016.

## Evidence (from arXiv HTML v2 full text)
- "the excess vocabulary during the Covid pandemic consisted almost entirely of content words (such as respiratory, remdesivir, etc.)"
- "whereas the excess vocabulary in 2024 consisted almost entirely of style words"
- "Out of all 319 excess style words in 2024, 66% were verbs and 16% were adjectives. For comparison, most excess words in prior years were nouns."

## Assessment
1. Quote support: The claim maps precisely onto the paper's own framing — content words (nouns, pandemic terms) vs style words (verbs/adjectives), the latter treated as LLM fingerprints.
2. Contradicting evidence: None found; this is the widely-cited primary result.
3. Source quality: Peer-reviewed Science Advances + arXiv preprint with public code (berenslab/llm-excess-vocab). Primary source matches claim strength.
4. Currency: 2025 publication, current for a fast-moving field.
5. Not marketing/press release; original peer-reviewed research.

# AI/LLM Writing Tells — Structural & Rhetorical Patterns (2024–2026)

Research angle: the *structural and rhetorical* markers of AI-generated English prose —
patterns of arrangement and argument that lexical/word-frequency scans (e.g. "delve",
"tapestry") tend to miss. Compiled from 2024–2026 sources.

## Rule of Three / Tricolon

The single most-cited structural tell. LLMs systematically deploy three-part structures
for emphasis — "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "short phrase, short phrase, and
short phrase" — and use them to make superficial analysis *look* comprehensive. Described
as "one of the most persistent structural patterns in AI-generated text."

- Wikipedia's field guide notes LLMs "overuse the rule of three."
- Two or three tricolons in a single paragraph is flagged as enough to identify GPT output
  in ~30 seconds by practitioner guides.
- Sources: gptzero.me/news/the-rule-of-three/, theaugmentededucator.com, en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing

## Negative Parallelism / Antithesis Constructions

A cluster of "negation → affirmation" rhetorical frames that recur heavily:

- **"Not only X, but Y"** — e.g. "not only a work of self-representation, but a visual document."
- **"It's not X, it's Y"** / "This isn't X — it's Y" — negation-affirmation pairs that
  restructure a claim through explicit rejection.
- **"X rather than Y"** — reversed parallelism (noted especially in Grok output), e.g.
  "prioritizing empirical consolidation rather than ideological purity."

These are treated as high-signal because they impose a rhetorical symmetry human writers
rarely reach for so consistently. (Source: Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing.)

## Predictable Sentence Templates

- **"From X to Y"** constructions.
- **Present-participial trailing clauses** ("..., revealing...", "..., highlighting...").
- Research cited by The Augmented Educator claims these appear at "two to five times the
  rate found in human-written text."

## Hedging & Vague Attribution ("weasel wording")

- Cautious throat-clearing: "it's important to note that," "generally speaking."
- Unsourced authority: "Industry reports," "Observers have cited," "Experts argue,"
  "Some experts say..." — opinions attributed to nebulous authorities without names or
  quantities. Framed as a safety-training artifact that suppresses definitive statements.
- Sources: theaugmentededucator.com, sh-reya.com/blog/ai-writing, Wikipedia.

## Structural Uniformity

- **Uniform paragraph length** and predictable sentence progression, lacking the varied
  pacing ("burstiness") of human writing.
- **Flat sentence rhythm** — uniform sentence length that diminishes engagement; human
  writers vary length to guide attention. (sh-reya.com)
- **Formulaic section templates**, e.g. rigid "Challenges and Legacy" sections:
  "Despite its [positive descriptor], [subject] faces [generic challenges]... Future
  investments... could enhance [subject's] efficiency." (Wikipedia)

## Formatting-as-Rhetoric Tells

- **Inline bold-header lists**: "**Construction and Renovation**: For cutting drywall..." —
  boldfaced inline headers with colons that mimic README/listicle conventions rather than
  prose.
- **Overuse of (nested) bullet lists** where connected prose is warranted. (sh-reya.com)

## Empty / Conclusory Framing

- **Empty conclusory sentences**: "By following these steps, we achieve better
  performance" — false closure that advances no thought.
- **Low information density**: polished, grammatically sound intros with "no concrete
  insight, no framing, no momentum" (Gemini example).
- **Demonstrative-pronoun overreliance** ("this," "that," "these") without clear
  antecedents. (sh-reya.com)

## How the tells have shifted (model evolution)

- Wikipedia's guide explicitly notes AI writing styles "shift in parallel with contemporary
  LLM tools across different years" — the field guide is versioned by model era.
- Em-dash usage reportedly increased with GPT-4.0 ("engineers tweaked this to include more
  em dashes"), turning the em-dash into a widely discussed but increasingly contested
  signal (see plagiarismtoday.com's test of whether it holds across systems).
- Practitioner consensus in 2025: single-signal detection (em-dash alone) is unreliable;
  *co-occurrence* of structural tells (tricolon + negative parallelism + uniform rhythm +
  hedging) is what remains discriminating as surface lexicon gets sanitized.

## Caveat: legitimate rhetoric mistakenly flagged

sh-reya.com cautions that intentional repetition, signposting, parallel structure,
predictable section headings, declarative openings, and em dashes are all rhetorically
sound when purposefully deployed — so these structural markers are probabilistic tells,
not proof.
